Optimization Guides

End-to-end guides for compressing, optimizing, and deploying AI models in production environments.

Guide 01 — Quantization

LLM Quantization Deep Dive: GPTQ, AWQ, and GGUF Compared

Quantization is the single most impactful optimization for deploying large language models. By reducing the precision of model weights from 16-bit floating point to 4-bit integers, you can reduce memory requirements by 4x and often achieve 2-3x faster inference speeds with minimal quality degradation. The three dominant quantization formats — GPTQ, AWQ, and GGUF — take different approaches to this precision reduction, and understanding their tradeoffs is essential for making the right choice for your deployment scenario.

GPTQ (GPT Quantization) uses a one-shot weight quantization approach based on approximate second-order information. It quantizes weights layer by layer, using the Hessian matrix to determine which weights are most sensitive to precision reduction and allocating more bits to those critical weights. GPTQ models are optimized for GPU inference and work best with frameworks like vLLM and ExLlamaV2. The main advantage is excellent quality preservation at 4-bit precision; the main limitation is that GPTQ models require a GPU for inference.

AWQ (Activation-Aware Weight Quantization) takes a different approach by identifying the most important weights based on activation patterns rather than weight magnitudes alone. This produces slightly better quality at the same bit-width in many benchmarks. GGUF, developed for the llama.cpp ecosystem, offers the broadest hardware compatibility — it runs on CPUs, Apple Silicon via Metal, NVIDIA GPUs via CUDA, and AMD GPUs via Vulkan. GGUF provides the most quantization levels (from Q2_K to Q8_0), letting you precisely balance model size against quality. For a comprehensive comparison of deployment platforms and tools, zovo.one maintains updated reviews across the optimization ecosystem.

Key Takeaway

Use GGUF for CPU/mixed inference and maximum hardware flexibility, GPTQ for dedicated GPU serving with vLLM, and AWQ when you need the best quality-at-bitwidth ratio on GPU. Always benchmark perplexity on your specific task after quantization — generic benchmarks do not predict domain-specific quality loss.

Guide 02 — Pruning

Practical Model Pruning: From Theory to 50% Sparsity

Pruning removes redundant or low-importance parameters from neural networks, reducing both memory footprint and computation requirements. The insight behind pruning is that trained neural networks are typically overparameterized — many weights contribute negligibly to the final output and can be removed (set to zero) without meaningful quality loss. The lottery ticket hypothesis suggests that within every large network exists a smaller subnetwork that could have achieved the same performance if trained independently.

Structured pruning removes entire neurons, attention heads, or layers, producing models that are immediately faster on standard hardware without requiring sparse computation support. Unstructured pruning removes individual weights regardless of their position, achieving higher compression ratios but requiring hardware or software that can exploit sparsity efficiently. For LLMs, the SparseGPT algorithm can achieve 50-60% unstructured sparsity in a single forward pass through the calibration data, without any retraining. The Wanda (Weights and Activations) method offers an even simpler approach using only weight magnitudes and input activations to determine pruning decisions.

Combining pruning with quantization (pruning then quantizing the remaining weights) yields the most aggressive compression ratios. A model that is 50% pruned and 4-bit quantized achieves roughly 8x total compression from the original FP16 representation. The challenge is measuring quality degradation accurately — perplexity scores can hide task-specific regressions, so always evaluate on benchmarks representative of your actual use case. AI workflow resources at ClaudHQ offer additional insights into managing complex optimization pipelines.

Key Takeaway

Start with SparseGPT or Wanda for LLM pruning — both achieve good sparsity without retraining. Use structured pruning for immediate speedups on standard GPUs, and combine pruning with quantization for maximum compression. Always measure quality on your specific tasks, not just perplexity.

Guide 03 — Distillation

Knowledge Distillation: Training Smaller Models That Punch Above Their Weight

Knowledge distillation transfers the learned behavior of a large teacher model into a smaller student model. The key insight is that the teacher's soft predictions (probability distributions over all classes, not just the argmax) contain rich information about the relationships between concepts that the student can learn from more efficiently than from the raw training data alone. A 7B parameter student trained with distillation from a 70B teacher often outperforms a 7B model trained from scratch on the same data.

For language models, distillation takes several forms. Logit distillation trains the student to match the teacher's output probability distributions, using a weighted combination of the standard cross-entropy loss and a distillation loss (KL divergence between student and teacher distributions). Feature distillation additionally matches intermediate layer representations, helping the student learn the teacher's internal representations. More recently, reasoning distillation has emerged as a technique where the teacher generates chain-of-thought reasoning traces that the student is trained to reproduce, transferring not just the answers but the reasoning process.

The practical workflow for distillation involves: selecting a teacher model with the capabilities you want, generating a large dataset of teacher outputs on your target domain, and fine-tuning the student on these outputs. The data generation step is critical — diversity and quality of the distillation dataset directly determine the student's capabilities. OpenAI's approach with GPT-4 teaching smaller models, and the open-source replication through projects like Alpaca and Vicuna, demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach at scale. Practitioners working with this technique may find additional workflow insights at ClaudKit.

Key Takeaway

Distillation is most effective when the student architecture is well-suited to your deployment constraints and the distillation dataset is diverse and high-quality. Invest in teacher output generation — a strong teacher with a weak dataset produces worse results than a moderate teacher with excellent data coverage.

Guide 04 — Edge

Edge Deployment: Running AI Models on Mobile and IoT Devices

Deploying AI models at the edge means running inference on devices like smartphones, tablets, embedded systems, and IoT hardware rather than in the cloud. Edge deployment eliminates network latency, reduces cloud compute costs, enables offline operation, and addresses data privacy concerns by keeping user data on-device. The tradeoff is that edge hardware has limited memory, compute power, and battery life, requiring aggressive model optimization.

The typical edge deployment pipeline starts with a trained model in PyTorch or TensorFlow, exports it to an intermediate representation like ONNX, applies optimization passes (operator fusion, constant folding, dead code elimination), quantizes to INT8 or lower, and converts to a device-specific format. For iOS, this means CoreML; for Android, TensorFlow Lite or ONNX Runtime Mobile; for NVIDIA Jetson devices, TensorRT. Each target runtime has different supported operations and optimization capabilities, so testing on the actual target device is essential.

Recent advances have made surprisingly capable models available at the edge. Apple's CoreML can run models with billions of parameters on iPhone and Mac through a combination of Neural Engine acceleration and memory-mapped model loading. Google's MediaPipe provides pre-optimized models for common tasks like pose estimation, face detection, and object recognition that run in real time on mobile devices. For custom models, the combination of ONNX export, INT8 quantization, and target-specific compilation typically achieves 5-10x latency reduction from the original floating-point PyTorch model.

Key Takeaway

Always profile on the target device — desktop benchmarks do not predict edge performance due to memory bandwidth, thermal throttling, and instruction set differences. Start with a proven architecture (MobileNet, EfficientNet) rather than trying to shrink a model designed for data center hardware.
